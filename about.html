SPACE

About

Big Bang and the Beginning of Space: The expansion of space traces back to the Big Bang, which wasn't an explosion in space, but rather the rapid expansion of space itself from an extremely hot, dense point about 13.8 billion years ago. This rapid expansion, called "cosmic inflation," happened faster than the speed of light and set the stage for the universe we see today.

Black holes are regions of space where gravity is so intense that nothing, not even light, can escape from them. They form when massive stars collapse under their own gravity at the end of their life cycles. The core becomes a singularity, a point of infinite density, while the surrounding region is the event horizon—the "point of no return." Black holes can range in size.

Neutron stars are the remnants of stars that have exploded in a supernova. What remains is an incredibly dense core composed almost entirely of neutrons. These stars are small (about 10-20 kilometers across) but incredibly dense—one teaspoon of neutron star material would weigh about a billion tons. Neutron stars also have incredibly strong magnetic fields and can rotate at speeds up to 600 times per second. Some neutron stars emit regular pulses of radiation, known as pulsars, which make them detectable across vast distances.

Quasars are incredibly bright and energetic objects found at the centers of some distant galaxies. They are powered by supermassive black holes that are actively feeding on surrounding gas and dust, releasing enormous amounts of energy as matter falls into the black hole. This process can outshine an entire galaxy, making quasars some of the most powerful objects in the universe.
